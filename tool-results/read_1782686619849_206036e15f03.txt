   101→## 3. MOMPOX (SANTA CRUZ DE MOMPOX / MOMPÓS) — TOWN PROFILE
   102→
   103→### 3.1 Essentials
   104→- **Official name:** Santa Cruz de Mompox (also Mompós; both valid)
   105→- **Department:** Bolívar, Caribbean Region, Colombia
   106→- **Coordinates:** 9°14′22″N 74°25′30″O
   107→- **Distance:** 248 km from Cartagena de Indias; ~6 h by road from Cartagena, Barranquilla; ~5h30 from Santa Marta; ~4h30 from Valledupar
   108→- **Population:** ~46,408 (municipality, 2020); ~23,649 urban • Elevation: 33 m • Area: 652.1 km²
   109→- **Geography:** A river-island town in the **Depresión Momposina**, formed by two arms (brazos) of the **Río Magdalena** — the Brazo de Mompox and the Brazo de Loba — which together enclose **Isla Margarita**, the largest island in continental Colombia.
   110→- **Titles:** *"Ciudad Valerosa"* (Courageous City) and *"Ciudad Culta"* (Cultured City); declared **Distrito Especial, Turístico, Histórico y Cultural** by Law 1875 of 2017.
   111→- **Current mayor (2024–2027):** Juan Sinning Ciodaro
   112→
   113→### 3.2 UNESCO World Heritage status (the headline credential)
   114→- **Inscribed:** 1995, 19th session of the World Heritage Committee
   115→- **Official name:** Historic Centre of Santa Cruz de Mompox
   116→- **Criteria:** Cultural (iv) and (v)
   117→- **Property:** 54.05 ha • **Buffer zone:** 237.48 ha • **Reference:** 742
   118→- **UNESCO brief synthesis (verbatim):** *"Founded in 1540 on the banks of the River Magdalena, Mompox played a key role in the Spanish colonization of northern South America. From the 16th to the 19th century the city developed parallel to the river, with the main street acting as a dyke. The historic centre has preserved the harmony and unity of the urban landscape. Most of the buildings are still used for their original purposes, providing an exceptional picture of what a Spanish colonial city was like."*
   119→- **Criterion (iv):** Integral to the processes of colonial penetration and dominion during the Spanish conquest, and to the growth of communications and commerce during the 17th to early 19th centuries.
   120→- **Criterion (v):** The city's free, longitudinal growth following the sinuous river path, followed by 19th-century economic decline, created a special quality with few regional parallels — making Mompox *"the region's most outstanding surviving example of this type of riverine urban settlement."*
   121→- **Other designations:** National Monument (Law 163 of 1959); Colombian **Pueblo Patrimonio** (2010, one of 11 original municipalities in the Red Turística de Pueblos Patrimonio); municipal building code since 1970 strictly controls all construction.
   122→- **Distinctive urban form:** Instead of a single central plaza, Mompox has **three plazas lined up along the river**, each with its own church, each corresponding to a former indigenous settlement. The main street doubled as a dyke (**albarradas** — barricade walls) against flooding. Houses on the Calle de la Albarrada are **"house-store" buildings** in rows of 3–10 units with open hallways across the front sharing a common roof. Private houses (17th–early 19th c.) are laid out around central or lateral open patios adapted to the climate.
   123→
   124→### 3.3 History (key chapters for storytelling)
   125→- **Pre-Columbian:** The **Cacique Mompoj** (Malibú culture; the name *Mompox* means "land of the ruler Mampo/Mompoj") governed ~50 small tribes (Güitacas, Chilloas, Chimíes, Chicaguas, Jaguas, Malibúes, Kates, Kimbayes, Menchiquejos, Talahiguas). Lieutenants: caciques Zuzua and Mahamon.
   126→- **Foundation:** **May 3, 1537** by **Alonso de Heredia** (brother of Pedro de Heredia, founder of Cartagena) — some sources credit Juan de Santa Cruz, 1540. Established as a safe port on the Magdalena.
   127→- **Colonial boom (16th–18th c.):** Prospered as a port moving goods upriver to the interior. A **royal mint** was established; the town was renowned for its **goldsmiths**. The **Convent of the Augustinian Hermits (17th c.)** brought a new era of progress (and the Inquisition). The **Jesuits founded the Colegio de San Carlos** (Latin grammar, philosophy, theology). **Hospital San Juan de Dios** was founded in 1540/1550 — the Knights Hospitaller took over in 1663; considered the oldest hospital in the Americas still functioning in its original building. Mompox was a **safe deposit for Spain's gold** and a refuge for wealthy families fleeing pirates on the coast. Notable residents: Conde de Santa Cruz de la Torre, Andrés de Madarriaga (hero of Cartagena's defense vs. Vernon), Juan Bautista de Mier y de la Torre (1st Marqués de Santa Coa, builder of Portales de la Marquesa), José Fernando de Mier y Guerra (pacifier of the Chimilas, founder of 20+ riverside towns). **Alexander von Humboldt** stayed at the Portales de la Marquesa on the Albarrada.
   128→- **Independence (1810):** On **August 6, 1810**, Mompox became the **first town in the New Kingdom of Granada to proclaim absolute independence from Spain**, under the motto *"Ser libres o morir"* (Be free or die). The flag (red with a white cross, officialized 1810 — coincidentally resembles the later Swiss flag of 1848) dates from this moment. The Act of Independence was signed at the City Hall.
   129→- **Simón Bolívar:** Stayed in Mompox in 1812; recruited **400 momposinos** for his **Campaña Admirable**, culminating in Caracas on August 6, 1813. His famous tribute: *"Si a Caracas debo la vida, a Mompox debo la gloria"* ("If to Caracas I owe my life, to Mompox I owe my glory"). Commemorated by the **Piedra de Bolívar** monument.
   130→- **Decline & preservation:** Early 20th century, sediment shifted the Magdalena's main flow to the **Brazo de Loba** (via Magangué); the Brazo de Mompox became stagnant, commerce collapsed, inhabitants emigrated. This isolation **preserved** the colonial city almost intact — the foundation of its 1995 UNESCO listing.
   131→- **Cultural cameo:** The 1987 film adaptation of **Gabriel García Márquez**'s *Crónica de una muerte anunciada* was partly shot in Mompox. García Márquez said towns like Mompox are *"where time doesn't pass"* — Mompox is widely cited as an inspiration for **Macondo**.

   133→### 3.4 Architecture & landmarks (showcase imagery goldmine)
   134→**The three signature streets** (all parallel to the river):
   135→- **Calle de la Albarrada** — the riverside street, with the dyke wall and 17th-c. "house-store" buildings; home to Iglesia de Santa Bárbara, Casa 1734, Portales de la Marquesa, and **Casa Mónica itself** (in the Santa Bárbara quarter)
   136→- **Calle Real del Medio** (Main Royal Street) — the most important street, concentrating the most captivating architecture: Casa de la Cultura (Residencia Peñas Alvarado), Bolívar & Santander Parks, Te Deum House, Justice Palace, Museum of Religious Art, Casa de los Apóstoles
   137→- **Calle de Atrás** — leads to Santo Domingo Church and the cemetery
   138→- Note: **Hotel Casa Mónica sits between Calle del Medio and Calle de Atrás**, i.e. between two of these three iconic streets, behind Iglesia Santa Bárbara.
   139→
   140→**The six (or seven) colonial churches:**
   141→1. **Iglesia de Santa Bárbara** (completed 1613) — the icon of Mompox. Baroque **octagonal bell tower** with a balcony; moldings of palm trees, flowers, and lions' heads; baroque dome; three heavily gilded altars. Legend: a Moorish princess Bárbara fell in love with a Christian youth; her father locked her in the tower and beheaded her; God struck him with lightning. Located on La Albarrada — **directly in front of Hotel Casa Mónica's neighborhood**.
   142→2. **Iglesia de la Inmaculada Concepción** — originally adobe by Pedro de Heredia (1541), enlarged 1551, often regarded as the cathedral; last restoration 1795; demolished 1839; present church built on site. On the main plaza.
   143→3. **Iglesia de San Francisco** — originally built 1564; convent founded 1580 by Fray Francisco Gonzaga. Oldest church in Mompox per some sources; white and ochre tones.
   144→4. **Iglesia de San Agustín** — 17th century (1606); the convent brought the Inquisition era.
   145→5. **Iglesia de San Juan de Dios** — attached to the 1550 hospital.
   146→6. **Iglesia de Santo Domingo** — on Calle de Atrás.
   147→7. (Some counts include the **Chapel of the Cemetery / Rosario**.)
   148→
   149→**Other key landmarks:**
   150→- **Cementerio Rosario de Mompox** (1829; chapel & altar 1846) — white tombs, many cats; pays homage to General Hermógenes Maza and the poet **Candelario Obeso**.
   151→- **Hospital San Juan de Dios** (1550) — oldest hospital in the Americas in its original building.
   152→- **City Hall** — colonial dungeons; Act of Independence signed here Aug 6, 1810.
   153→- **Municipal Palace / Cloister of San Carlos** (1660) — first secondary school; Jesuits expelled; Universal School of Saint Peter the Apostle founded 1809 by Pedro Martínez de Pinillos.
   154→- **Casa de la Cultura** (Residencia Peñas Alvarado) — colonial residence, cultural exhibits; honors poet Candelario Obeso.
   155→- **Portales de la Marquesa** — mansion of the Marqués de Santa Coa; where Humboldt stayed.
   156→- **Casa de los Apóstoles** (Calle Real del Medio) — images of the 12 apostles + Last Supper.
   157→- **Hostal Doña Manuela** — impressive multicentennial **ficus tree**.
   158→- **Mompox farmer's market** — turn-of-20th-c. building.
   159→- **Piedra de Bolívar** — monument to Bolívar's stay.
   160→- **Statue of Liberty** — in front of the Cloister of San Carlos.
   161→- **Bolívar Stone, Te Deum House** — frequently cited nearby points.

   163→**Architectural vocabulary** (useful for the showcase site's design language): rammed-earth walls (**tierra de embudo/tapial**), clay-tile roofs, **wrought-iron window grills, railings and door hardware** (the signature Mompox ironwork), thick walls, enormous interior patios, wood balconies, towering ceilings, ceiling fans, rocking chairs (**silla mecedora momposina**), white-washed facades, cobblestone streets, ceiba trees.

   165→### 3.5 Living traditions & culture
   166→- **Filigrana Momposina (filigree)** — Mompox's signature craft. 16th-century artisan guilds (clay workers, blacksmiths, goldsmiths, silversmiths) + gold trade from Antioquia + African + indigenous techniques → a 300-year-old tradition of weaving fine gold/silver threads into jewels with spiral motifs. Now often silver due to gold prices. World-famous; workshops open to visitors; visitors can take a 3-hour hands-on workshop and craft their own silver piece. **Joyas de la Villa** and many family workshops are the local references.
   167→- **Semana Santa (Holy Week)** — first celebrated in **1564**; the **second most important Holy Week in Colombia after Popayán**. Fusion of Catholic devotion with magical/pagan elements. Key events:
   168→  - **Serenata a los Difuntos** (Serenade to the Dead): Good Wednesday afternoon at the municipal cemetery — inhabitants in best clothing sit beside relatives' graves from 6 PM into the small hours, lit by candles, decorated with flowers, to the rhythm of funeral music.
   169→  - **Processions** of the **seven colonial churches**; the Good Thursday & Good Friday marches use a unique **"two steps forward, one step back"** rhythm — solemn and imposing.
   170→  - **Desprendimiento** (Detachment) & **Paso Robado** (Stolen Effigy): midnight Good Friday at San Francisco church — parishioners knock on the three doors; the effigy is "stolen," hidden until 2 AM, then "recovered" by nazarenes and carried to Santo Domingo to a military-type march composed for the occasion.
   171→  - Greatest tourist influx of the year; book accommodations well in advance.
   172→- **Mompox Jazz Festival** (Festival Mompox Festijazz) — annual, September/October.
   173→- **Festival Totó la Momposina** — July 19–21, honoring the legendary singer (recently deceased at 85).
   174→- **Festival de Cultura Semana Santa** and **Semana Santica**.
   175→- **Mompox music:** Cumbia, bullerengue, porro, mapalé, chalupas, garabatos, sones, guarachas. **Totó la Momposina** (Sonia Bazanta Vides) is the icon; **Candelario Obeso** is the literary icon (*Cantos populares de mi tierra*, precursor of "poesía negra y oscura"). The **Cumbión** is a town-wide parade. The traditional "Bailes cantados de Mompox" — cumbia preserved by local folk groups — is offered as a nocturnal experience.

   208→- **Hot season:** Jan 25 – Apr 13 (daily high > 97°F). **Cool season:** Sep 18 – Nov 30 (daily high < 90°F).
   209→- **Rain:** wetter season Apr 15 – Nov 26 (peak **October**, ~14 wet days, 3.9 in / 99 mm). Drier season Nov 26 – Apr 15 (driest **January**, 1.5 wet days, 0.3 in / 8 mm). Rainless period: Dec 30 – Mar 13.

   212→- **Sun:** earliest sunrise 5:36 AM (May 28); latest sunset 6:22 PM (Jul 13).
   213→- **Best time to visit:** **mid-December to late February** (peak second week of January) for both general outdoor tourism and hot-weather/beach-pool activities. Avoid peak crowds in January high season, Easter week, and the Jazz Festival (Sep/Oct) — book ahead in those windows.

   216→### 3.8 How to get to Mompox
   217→**By air:**
   218→- **San Bernardo Airport (Mompox)** — closest, but **no permanent routes**; active only during **Semana Santa** and the **Jazz Festival**.
   219→- **Satena** operates a **direct Medellín (Olaya Herrera) → Mompox flight twice weekly** (useful from the south).
   220→- Year-round alternative: **Las Brujas Airport, Corozal (Sucre, CZU)** — flights from Bogotá, Medellín, Barranquilla, Bucaramanga; then by land to Magangué and across the new bridge.
   221→
   222→**By land:**
   223→- **From Cartagena:** 3 direct daily buses with **Unitransco**, ~6 h, direct.
   224→- **From Santa Marta:** 1 daily **Copetran** bus, ~9 h (not ideal).
   225→- **From Bogotá:** day and night buses via El Banco or direct.
   226→- **From El Carmen de Bolívar:** take the **Ruta del Sol III**, detour at **La Gloria (Magdalena)**, through **Santa Ana** and **Talaigua Nuevo** to Mompox.
   227→- **Game-changer (2020):** the **Puente Roncador / "Reconciliación" bridge** (Yatí–Bodega segment), ~12 km over the Magdalena, **inaugurated March 30, 2020** — part of the Ruta del Sol, reducing travel time between the interior and the Caribbean coast by ~3 hours and finally connecting Mompox by land without ferries.
   228→- **No central bus terminal** in Mompox; each company has its own small point of sale along the main road on the outskirts; from there a **tuk-tuk (5 min)** reaches the historic center.
   229→
   230→**By water:**
   231→- The **Brazo de Mompox** of the Magdalena; Mompox has a **river terminal** linked to the old port, with vessels from **Magangué, El Banco, Santa Ana, Barrancabermeja**.
   232→- The historic route: Cartagena → Magangué → ferry/chalupa across the Magdalena to La Bodega → road to Mompox through plantations and cattle haciendas (now superseded by the bridge).
   233→
   234→**Getting around Mompox:**
   235→- **On foot** — the best way; the historic center is compact and cobblestoned.
   236→- **Tuk-tuk** — cheap, ubiquitous, fun; for longer hops or tired feet.
   237→- **Boat** — public metal boats cross the river to the farming communities where Mompox's vegetables are grown; chickens, grandmothers, fruit and even motorcycles pile on board.
